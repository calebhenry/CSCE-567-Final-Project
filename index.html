Exoplanet Report

This website exists to inform those interested in astronomy about how we find exoplanets, how that has changed over time, and the results of those observations. All data on this page can be found here at the NASA Exoplanet Archive.

Facility Information

This is a a set of visualizations that show information about the facilities that observe exoplanets. You can interact with these by clicking through the two tabs, clicking on parts of the legend, as well as hovering over points.There are a few key takeaways here. Firstly, we can see that the vast majority of these observations are made from space, specifically using the transit method, which tracks stars dimming as a planet passes in front of them. We also see that the only method used by space telescopes is tracking transits, and all other methods are done from the ground. This leads us to believe that space telescopes are responsible for most observations. The Kepler Space Telescope is responsible for a magnitude more planetary discoveries than pretty much anything else. We see that K2 and TESS, two other telescopes, also peak off the axis, but nothing near the scale of Kepler. The final thing we see for this is that the scopes can see a wide variety of spectral types.

Stellar Information

This is a a set of visualizations that show information about stars, the planets that orbit them, and how we have gotten better at finding them over time. You can interact with these by clicking through the three tabs, clicking on parts of the legend, as well as hovering over points. There are a few key takeaways here. Firstly, we have gotten better and better at seeing smaller stars and planets over time, most likely due to better technology. We also see that the combination of one planet and one star is the most common, and that a system is likely to have more planets than stars. It is also evident that there is a positive correlation between the mass of a star and the temperature of the star, as well as between the size of a star/planet and the length of time it takes to transit (pass in front of the star).

Galactic Map

Below is a map of every system in the galaxy that we have discovered planets orbiting. By manipulating the sidebar, you can view different spectral types, as well as expand the range of the map. The orange dot is Earth, and the light blue dots are other systems. I highly recommend expanding the map to the maximum range to see the overall patterns. You will notice that the planets seem to move out from Earth in spokes, and that is because we can only see the planets if they are at a particular angle in reference to Earth. The particularly long one extends along the galactic disk, which is more dense compared to the others. When you see galactic x, y, and z, that refers to the distance in light years the star is in that direction.
